The map shows percentage deviation from average annual rainfall, and highlights major droughts and their agricultural impact.

If a drought occurs only during the growing season, it will severely affect food production but might not show up in the yearly averages. Similarly, as each data point is 1° resolution (equivalent to 100km around the equator), a region-specific drought within a country might be lost.

In desert regions, large percentage deviation from average rainfall represent small absolute changes and have little impact on agriculture and human society.

2012

Drought spreads to northeast Brazil, bringing water shortages to the eastern state of Bahia. Twenty million people are affected as over 900 municipalities declare states of emergency73.

In the region around the Black Sea, drought destroys tens of millions of hectares of wheat70. Grain exports drop by 27%71, with damage to crops worth US$1 billion in Russia alone72.

Drought spreads from the southwest throughout the United States69. At its peak in late summer, 81% of the contiguous United States is under “abnormally dry” conditions or worse. Maize is hit hard, with harvest 75% of that anticipated64.

2011

In China, rainfall is down 60% on 50-year average in the Yangtze basin. Millions of people and livestock go without drinking water and millions of hectares of crops are destroyed68.

In Argentina and Brazil, drought causes yield losses of 40% in some maize-growing regions66. Sugar cane losses reach 30% in some parts of Brazil and soy yield declines in both countries67.

Coming after two successive poor rainy seasons, severe drought in East Africa results in loss of around 50% of crops and livestock. Between 50,000 and 100,000 people die across Somalia, Ethiopia and Kenya65.

Texas experiences its driest 12 months ever. At one point, 80% of the state is rated as having an “exceptional” level of drought64.

2010

In Russia, severe drought in the key growing months of June and July seriously damages agriculture, eventually destroying a third of crops62. Russia bans grain exports in August, driving up global food prices63.

China’s southwest Yunnan province feels the effects of its worst drought in decades. By May, more than 8 million people — 18% of Yunnan's population — are short of drinking water61.

A severe drought in the Amazon — the second in five years — causes as much damage to the rainforest as a year’s worth of human-led deforestation60. Scientists theorize that the drought is caused by climate change warming the waters in the Atlantic Ocean to the north of the equator60.

2009

Rainfall in the Caribbean is the lowest in its 25 years of records. This leads to drought throughout 2010, reducing the banana harvest on Dominica by 43%, agricultural production in Saint Vincent and the Grenadines by 20%, and onion and tomato yields in Antigua and Barbuda by 25–30%58.

The Australian ‘Big Dry’ continues, encompassing a third consecutive year of a particularly severe drought in the states of Victoria and New South Wales. The drought only breaks when La Niña rains come in late 201056. But the country is not declared officially drought free until 2012, by which time economic assistance to farmers has cost US$4.2 billion57.

India experiences severe drought due to failed monsoon. In Andhra Pradesh, the ‘rice bowl’ state, rainfall over the monsoon season is 29% below average and the rice harvest is predicted to drop by 10 million tonnes — or roughly 10%55.

2008

The ‘Big Dry’ persists1, particularly in southeastern Australia. The Murray-Darling river basin dries out completely and rice production falls to 2% of pre-drought levels54.

Two successive seasons of poor rains leave eastern Ethiopia in drought52. By the end of May, food and cash crops such as coffee are failing. Food prices soar 500% in some parts of the country. An estimated 4.6 million people in the region need food aid by September53.

2007

During Summer and Autumn, the southeastern United States, including Georgia, Alabama, Tennessee and Florida, suffer severe drought carried over from the winter of 2005–2006 50. By October, many cities are close to running out of water51.

Continuing Australia’s ‘Big Dry’, parts of the country, including the Murray-Darling river basin, experience one of the driest April on record. The government is paying out US$1.7 million a day in drought relief to farmers48. In May, however, an unusual El Niño weather pattern brings rain and at least temporary relief to southern and central parts of the country49.

2006

In the UK, the 21 months ending July 2006 have severely low rainfall, especially across two successive winter–spring periods. Coupled with higher than average temperatures this leads to one of the country’s worst ever droughts. The Thames river basin sees its largest water shortage in over 100 years, with 13 million people affected by water restrictions46.

The Australian ‘Big Dry’ enters its 6th year1. As summer begins, the Murray-Darling river system is already less than half of its previous record minimum. The government forecasts its lowest wheat crop for more than a decade, down two-thirds on the 2005 crop47.

2005

Year 5 of Australia’s ‘Big Dry’, compounded by El Niño1 and the drought intensifies as the first five months of the year — vital for agriculture — are exceptionally dry37.

Southwest Amazonia is hit with one of its most intense droughts of the last 100 years. Caused by unusual weather patterns including a very warm tropical North Atlantic (unrelated to El Niño). Soybean and maize production fall by 65% and 55% respectively, river levels fall to a historic low and navigation is suspended along parts of Amazon1 44.

Several years of failing rains coupled with conflict brings drought to the Horn of Africa, which leads to severe food shortages in 2006 as more than 11 million people need food assistance. Effects of the drought are especially severe in pastoral areas of northern and eastern Kenya, eastern and southern Ethiopia, south Somalia and Djibouti, where there are major crop failures45.

2004

Year 4 of Australia’s ‘Big Dry’, compounded by El Niño1. As in 2003, temperatures are high and rainfall too sparse to replenish stores37.

In India, the annual monsoon starts well but breaks suddenly in July, bringing drought to many states and severely damaging agricultural productivity. Massive shortfalls in rice, cotton, coarse cereals (such as millet) and milk production follow38.

Drought hits Niger, affecting both northern regions and the country’s prime agricultural and pastoral zone in the south41. Combined with a severe locust invasion, this precipitates a major food crisis. By mid-2005 an estimated 800,000 under-fives are affected42 43.

2003

Drought in Siberia leads to wildfires, which burn millions of hectares of peat lands that are used for fertilizer and energy production1.

The most severe heatwave in 500 years descends on Europe, with temperatures above 40°C breaking records in many countries. Ensuing droughts, heat stress and fires cause 37,000 human fatalities and damages of US$15 billion to farming, livestock and forestry industries1 39 40.

Year 3 of Australia’s ‘Big Dry’, compounded by El Niño1. Temperatures are still high and rains in the first half of the year are average at best37.

2002

Long-term drought in Mongolia, which began in 1999, now covers 70% of the country. Lakes dry up and an estimated 12 million livestock die. Thought to be caused by ongoing declines in spring precipitation and rising temperatures1.

Year 2 of Australia’s ‘Big Dry’, compounded by El Niño, is one of the worst on record. Farm production is down by more than a quarter1 36. For most of the country, the 11 months leading to January 2003 have below average rainfall and above average temperatures. GDP drops by more than 1%.37.

Widespread drought occurs in India, as monsoon rainfall is half the average for July. Reservoirs are at lowest level in a decade. Drought hits 300 million people, 1 million cattle and 30% of the total crop area1 38.

The drought in western North America reaches its peak in the summer, covering about 41% of the continent from the Canadian prairies to northern Mexico. It is thought to be the worst in the region for 800 years1 33 34. In Canada, losses from agriculture exceed US$3.6 billion for 2001–02 and GDP falls by US$5.7 billion35.

2001

2001 marks the first year of the Millennium Drought, or ‘Big Dry’, in eastern Australia. Lasting until 2009, it is described as the region’s worst ever drought1. Impacts include widespread crop failures, livestock losses, dust storms, and bushfires.

Drought persists in northern China, depriving 33 million people and 22 million head of livestock of drinking water and causing crop damages in excess of US$6 billion1.

Drought continues in North America spreading east to include some of the eastern seaboard, destroying forests and depleting river basins1 33 34.

2000

Western North America experiences particularly low rainfall, which is to persist for another 2 years1 33.

Failure of rains in eastern Africa brings widespread drought, most notably to Ethiopia. Many families lose their cattle, which are vital for subsistence particularly in the south of the country. In total, around 8 million people are at risk, including 1.4 million children under the age of five32.

Continuation of severe drought in northern China from previous year. More than 400,000 km2 of agricultural land damaged1.

1999

Regional drought affects Argentina, notably the agriculturally productive northeast. Below average rainfall in spring and summer results in failure of most crops and damage to fruit trees31.

Low rainfall in the summer of 1998, persisting until May 1999, causes drought in Mauritius that reduces sugar cane production by around 50% 29. In neighbouring Madagascar, drought in late 1999 reduced yields of rice, maize and export crops such as vanilla, coffee, cloves and peppers30.

In northern Mexico, drought worsens as spring rainfall drops 93% below average for this time of year27. The country sees an overall 2% reduction in total crop production, and the largest soybean producer, the state of Zacatecas, loses 60% of its crop28.

Drought hits northern China, causing dust storms and water shortages but mostly affecting agriculture1.

1998

The southern United States and Central America succumb to drought yet again. Mexico experiences its worst drought in 70 years. Fires destroy nearly 2,000 km2 in Florida27, while the total cost of the drought is estimated to be $7.5 billion3.

Drought hits the Philippines from July 1997 through September 1998, causing widespread famine. Up to 3.5 million people in the southern island of Mindanao need emergency food supplies26.

1997

Papua New Guinea and the Solomon Islands badly affected by El Niño-related drought. Impacts include: lack of clean drinking water for hundreds of thousands; fire damage to grasslands and forest; an increase in disease transmission; and the closure of airstrips due to smoke pollution25.

One of the strongest El Niño events ever seen occurs in 1997–98, setting record temperatures across the world and bringing widespread drought, flooding and numerous other adverse effects24.

In New Zealand, drought from the record El Niño causes losses of US$620 million to the economy1.

Farmland covering more than 8.7 million km2 in China and Southeast Asia is damaged by El Niño-related drought1.

1996

Drought develops in the United States in the southwestern and southern plains states and in northern Mexico. It starts towards the end of 1995, and by May 1996 covers most of the south of the United States. Many of the region’s crops and pastures are destroyed or damaged, including almost all of New Mexico’s non-irrigated crops of winter wheat and sorghum24.

1995

Low rainfall and unusually high temperatures in the spring and summer of 1995 bring drought to England and Wales20. The quality and yield of potatoes, sugar beet and field vegetables are reduced, while livestock need supplemental feed to compensate for poor-quality pastures21. Dry, cool winters follow warm, dry summers, depleting reservoirs and leading to hydrological drought by 199822.

Lengthy Spanish drought now estimated to have cost more than US$4.9 billion, mostly in agricultural damage1.

1994

Low rainfall in July brings drought to East Asia, especially China, South Korea and Japan18. In China, which has recently experienced a string of major droughts, 82 million people are affected and the cost of damage exceeds US$13 billion10.

Drought starts in Queensland and New South Wales then spreads to eastern Tasmania and South Australia in the Autumn. This leads to widespread crop failure, with wheat crop in the eastern states worst affected and rural production declining by 10%2.

Drought in New Zealand leads to water supply crisis to the city of Auckland, where reservoirs reach a low of 32% capacity in May19.

Drought continues in Spain1.

1993

Summer drought conditions affect the southeastern United States, claiming 16 lives with an estimated cost of US$1 billion. In Autumn, drought and high winds bring wildfires to southern California, costing an addition US$1 billion and claiming 4 lives3.

Dry weather conditions over the semi-arid northeast region of Brazil, persisting since 1990, worsen — bringing severe drought to the region and forcing millions of people to the brink of starvation16 17.

The African drought continues, spreading down to South Africa. At 21 months, it is the continent’s longest drought on record1.

Drought continues in Spain1.

Turkish drought ends, having stimulated the development of rain enhancement projects across the country1.

1992

Drought affects 7.3 million km2 of Africa, beginning across Zambia, Zimbabwe and Mozambique1.

European drought persists, with Spain the worst affected country. In 1992 alone, the value of the cereal crop harvest in Spain is halved1.

Drought continues in Turkey1.

1991

Prolonged drought hits Zimbabwe, bringing water and electricity shortages to the country and reducing manufacturing productivity by nearly 10%13.

Californian drought is now one of worst on record. In an effort to mitigate the socio-economic impacts, the state Department of Water Resources buys water from farmers, landlords and agencies in northern and central California and resells it to urban and agricultural areas8 15.

Most of Europe affected by drought1.

Another dry winter elongates the drought in Turkey1 14.

1990

This fourth year of a 5-year drought in California is the driest yet. Snowmelt in spring is at 10% of normal level, and reservoirs run low. Farms in California’s Central Valley that rely on the Bureau of Reclamation for irrigation water see their supplies cut by up to 50%, for just the second time since the 1930s12.

Poor winter rainfall extends the drought in Turkey. The southeastern Anatolia region is particularly hard hit with decreased flow of the Euphrates river11.

Siberian drought now covers more than 3 million km2 and has lasted for 42 months, making it the longest drought ever recorded1.

1989

Drought begins in Turkey. Unreliable rainfall in the centre and southeast of the country damages agriculture1.

Dry conditions continue from the previous year for a number of US states including Iowa, Missouri, eastern Nebraska, Kansas and Colorado, and for some regions in Canada3.

Most of Europe affected by drought1.

Drought in Siberia persists, mainly over the basin of the river Ob1.

California still in drought8.

1988

The United States and Canada jointly experience their worst drought since the 1950s. Damage to energy, water and agricultural systems — as well as to the environment — cost US$39 billion in the US and US$1.8 billion in western Canada1.

California still in drought8.

Drought in Siberia is spreading west1.

An intense heat wave brings on another in the series of droughts in China9, affecting 49 million people and killing 1,40010.

1987

India experiences one of the worst droughts of the 20th century. Rainfall decreases by an average of twenty percent. The greatest impacts seen in the dry northwest and in Pakistan. Around 285 million people are affected by food shortages.1.

California still in drought8.

Drought persists in Siberia1.

1986

The southeast US sees a shortlived but intense drought from June through August, the key growth period for soybean and maize. Estimated losses from agriculture are US$1.3 billion and there are 100 human mortalities3.

In California, 1986 marks the start of a five-year drought8.

Drought starts in eastern Siberia1.

Between 1986 and 1994, China experiences six major drought events. Lasting up to several months at a time, these droughts mostly affect the north of the country, which grows wheat, millet and soybean1.

1985

The Sahel drought now affects 21 countries in sub-Saharan Africa, but only a handful of these countries suffer famine. One of these few is Ethiopia. Estimates of human mortality range from 600,000 up to 1 million16 7.

1984

Drought in Botswana has reduced the national cattle herd by 20%4.

Drought continues in the Sahel, peaking at 8.2 million km2 1. In parts of Ethiopia, including the area that is now Eritrea, 1984 is one of the driest years on record, exacerbating the existing famine6.

Drought in northeast Africa correlates with a strong El Niño. Low streamflow in the Blue Nile river reduces agricultural production and depletes water supply for downstream nations such as Sudan and Egypt.1

1983

Botswana drought continues4.

Africa’s Sahel region, which spreads from Senegal in the West to Eritrea in the East, experiences a series of long-term dry periods in the 1970s and 1980s. Peak drought extent is in 1983, covering 65% of the region1. Famine begins in Ethiopia, driven by conflict and poor government policy as much as by drought66

The Australian drought peaks in February. The lack of rainfall coupled with temperatures above 40 °C results in dry and hot conditions in southeast Australia and Tasmania. On 16 February, 180 separate bushfires break out, earning the day the name Ash Wednesday. The fires claim 75 lives1.

1982

Botswana drought continues4.

A two-year drought begins in Niger, which reduces national cattle herd by 62%5.

A prolonged drought begins in eastern Siberia, causing an increase in wildfires. It covers almost 6 million km2 and lasts for 23 months1.

A severe drought starts in New Zealand, lasting through 1983 and the first half of 1984, mainly affecting the country’s prime industry of pastoral agriculture1.

The El Niño-driven drought in Australia enters its fourth year. In the southeastern states of Victoria and South Australia, the drought is most severe between April 1982 and January 1983. Ranking as one of the worst droughts to hit the country, economic costs reach US$3 billion. Impacts on agriculture are harsh , leading to rural unemployment11.

1981

Botswana sees the onset of a 4-year drought4.

Third year of a four-year drought in eastern Australia2.

1980

From June to September 1980, an intense heatwave descends on the central and Midwestern US. Damage costs are estimated at around $US20 billion. 10,000 people die from drought-related impacts and heat stress3.

Second year of drought in eastern Australia2.

1979

Severe drought hits in the humid regions — in the south and west of India. Rainfall is 19% below average levels and food production is down 20%1.

Following an exceptionally wet decade, a 4-year drought begins in eastern Australia, driven by El Niño2.
