Drought spreads to northeast Brazil in early 2012, bringing water shortages to Bahia. 4 million people are affected with over 900 municipalities declaring a state of emergency.

In Russia and the Black Sea Region, drought in 2012 destroys tens of millions of hectares of wheat and causes damage worth 1 billion dollars in Russia alone.

Drought spreads from the southwest throughout the US. At its peak in 2012, an astounding 81 percent of the contiguous US is under at least abnormally dry conditions. Corn crop is hit hard, with harvest 75 percent of that anticipated.

In China, rainfall in 2011 is down 60% on 50-year average in some regions. Millions of people and livestock go without drinking water and millions of hectares of crops are destroyed.

In Argentina and Brazil, drought causes yield losses of 40% in some corn-growing regions; Sugar cane losses reach 30% in some parts of Brazil and soy yield declines in both countries.

Severe drought in the Horn of Africa in 2011 results in loss of around 50% of crops and livestock, and human mortalities of 50,000-100,000.

Texas experiences its driest 12 months ever. At one point, 80 percent of the state is rated as having an “exceptional” level of drought.

In Russia, severe drought in June and July seriously damages crops, 32% of which are destroyed by the end of July. Russia bans grain exports in August, driving up global food prices.

China’s Yunnan province feels the effects of its worst drought in decades. By May more than 8 million people — 18% of Yunnan's population — are short of drinking water, and US$2.5-billion worth of crops are expected to fail.

A severe drought in the Amazon – the second in five years – causes as much damage to the forest as a year’s worth of man-made deforestation. Scientists say it may be caused by water temperatures in the Atlantic Ocean north of the equator heating up with climate change.

Drought throughout the Caribbean in 2009 and 2010 reduces the banana harvest on Dominica by 43 percent, agricultural production in Saint Vincent and the Grenadines by 20 percent below the historic average, and onion and tomato yields in Antigua and Barbuda by 25–30 percent.

2009 marks the third consequetive year of severe drought in the Australian states of Victoria and New South Wales. The drought finally breaks when La Niña rains come in late 2010.

India experiences severe drought due to failed monsoon. In Andhra Pradesh, the state that produces much of India’s rice, rainfall over the monsoon season is 43% below average.

Continued drought in southeastern Australia causes Murray-Darling river basin to dry out and rice production to fall to two percent of pre-drought levels.

Two successive seasons of poor rains leave eastern Ethiopia in drought. By the end of May, food and cash crops such as coffee are failing. Food prices soar 500 percent in some parts of the country. An estimated 4.6 million people need food aid in the region by September.

During summer and autumn of 2007, the southeastern United States, including Georgia, Alabama, Tennessee and Florida suffer severe drought carried over from the winter of 2005/6. By October 2007, many cities are close to running out of water.

By the end of April, most of Australia is in drought, the result of an unusual El Nino weather pattern. Parts of the country such as the Murray Darling River Basin experience their driest April on record. By May, rainfall brings at least temporary relief to much of southern and central Australia

Drought coupled with conflict brings severe food shortages to the Horn of Africa. More than 11 million people are left needing food assistance. Effects of the droughts are especially severe in pastoral areas of northern and eastern Kenya, south-eastern Ethiopia and Djibouti, where there are major crop failures.

From 2004-6, the UK experiences one of the worst droughts in its history. Severe rainfall shortages occur over the 21 months ending in July 2006. Rainfall shortages greatest in winter and spring. The Thames River basin sees its largest water shortage in over 100 years.

Southwestern Amazonia is hit with one of the most intense droughts of the last hundred years. Caused by unusual weather patterns including a very warm tropical North Atlantic. River levels fall to historic low levels and navigation suspended along parts of Amazon.

A late and weak monsoon brings drought to many Indian states, causing severe damage to the country’s agricultural productivity. Massive shortfalls in rice, cotton, coarse cereals and milk production follow

Drought hits the south of Niger, the country’s prime agricultural and pastoral zone. Combined with a severe locust invasion, this precipitates a major food crisis. By mid-2005 an estimated 800 000 children under-five are facing famine.

Drought in Siberia leads to wildfires, burning millions of acres of peat lands.

The most severe heatwave in 500 years descends on Europe, with temperatures above 40°C breaking records in many countries. Ensuing droughts, heat stress and fires cause 35,000 human fatalities and damages of $15 billion to farming, livestock and forestry industry.

Continuing from 2000, major drought spreads across the Canadian prairies, concentrated on Alberta and Saskatchewan. Losses from agriculture exceed $3 billion and GDP falls by almost $6 billion.

Long-term drought in Mongolia from 1999-2002 has huge impacts on livestock and water resources. An estimated 12 million livestock die in total, and lakes dry up over 50-70 percent of the country. Thought to be caused by declining spring precipitation and rising temperatures.

Widespread drought occurs throughout India, as monsoon rainfall is 56% below average for July. An estimated 300 million people and 30% of the total crop area are affected.

2001 marks the first year of what becomes known as the Millennium Drought or ‘Big Dry’ in southeastern Australia. Lasting until 2009, it is said to be the regions’ worst ever drought. Impacts include widespread crop failures, livestock losses, dust storms, and bushfires.

Continued drought in northern China leads to the cessation of drinking water for 33 million rural people and 22 million livestock, and causes crop damages in excess of US$6 billion.

Chronic drought hits western North America from 2000 until 2004, destroying forests and depleting river basins. Thought to be the worst in the region for 800 years.

Widespread drought impacts large parts of Ethiopia. More than 8 million people need food assistance, including 1.4 million children under the age of five.

Severe drought in northern China continues from 1999 and lasts through to 2002. In 2000, over 40 million hectares of agricultural land damaged

Regional droughts affect regions such as Northeastern Argentina and southern California.

Prompted by low rainfall the previous summer, drought in Mauritius reduces sugar cane production by around 50 percent. Drought ends in May 1999. In neighbouring Madagascar, drought in late 1999 reduced yields of rice and maize.

In northern Mexico, drought worsens as spring rainfall drops 93% below the average level.

The southern US succumbs to drought yet again. Fires destroy more than 475,000 acres in Florida and cost US$500 million. Mexico experiences its worst drought in 70 year.

Drought hits the Philippines from July 1997 through September 1998, causing widespread famine. Up to three-and-a-half million people in the southern island of Mindanao need emergency food supplies.

Papua New Guinea and the Solomon Islands badly affected by El-Nino related drought. Impacts include lack of clean drinking water for hundreds of thousands; fire damage to grasslands and forest, an increase in disease transmission, and the closure of airstrips due to smoke pollution.

One of the strongest El Nino events on record occurs in 1997 and 1998, setting record temperatures across the globe and bringing widespread drought, flooding and numerous other ill effects. Over 8.7 million km2 throughout Asia damaged by drought.

Drought in New Zealand starting in 1997 extends into 1998 and causes losses of US$620 million to the economy.

Beginning in late 1995, drought develops in the southwestern US, southern plains states, and northern Mexico. By May 1996 drought covers much of the southern US. Many of the region's crops and pastures are destroyed or damaged, including New Mexico’s unirrigated winter wheat and sorghum crops.

Low rainfall and record temperatures in spring-summer of 1995 bring drought throughout England and Wales, stressing water supplies. Drought persists into 1998 as dry, cool winters follow warm, dry summers, depleting water levels in reservoirs.

Low rainfall in July brings drought to East Asia, especially China, South Korea and Japan. In China, 82 million people are affected and the cost of damage exceeds $13 billion.

Drought in Queensland and New South Wales, Australia spreads to eastern Tasmania and South Australia in autumn 1994. This leads to widespread crop failure, with wheat crop in the eastern states worst affected. Rural production declined by 10 percent in1994–95 as a result of the drought.

Summer drought conditions affect the southeastern US, claiming 16 lives and costing US$1 billion in damages. In Autumn, drought and high winds bring wildfires to California, costing an addition billion dollars.

Dry weather conditions over north-east Brazil persisting since 1990 worsen, bringing severe drought to the region. Millions of people are forced to the brink of starvation.

Between 1986 and 1994, 6 separate droughts hit China. Lasting up to several months each, these droughts mostly affected the north of the country.

Drought affects 7.3 million km2 of Africa during 1992-93, lasting 21 months in total. The 1992 Southern African drought was the region's worst drought in living memory.

In 1992, a long-term drought settled on Europe that persisted until 1995. Spain was the region worst affected, with damages in excess of €3.7 billion mostly in agricultural damage. In 1992 alone, the value of cereal crops in Spain was almost half the average.

In 1991 to 1992, prolonged drought hits Zimbabwe, bringing water and electricity shortages to the country and a reducing manufacturing productivity by 9.5 percent.

In an effort to mitigate the socio-economic impacts of a five-year drought, the state of California department of Water Resources buys water from farmers, landlords, and agencies in northern and central California and resells it to urban and agricultural areas.

Drought in Europe brings water shortages to Italy, Spain and the UK.

1990 is the driest year of a four-year drought in California. Spring snowmelt is at 10 percent of the average level, and water levels decline in reservoirs.

Starting in 1989, Turkey has a major national drought that extends until 1994. Unreliable rainfall in the centre and southeast of the country affects agriculture and leads to the development of national rain-enhancement projects.

Dry conditions continue from the previous year for a number of US states including Iowa, Missouri, eastern Nebraska, Kansas and Colorado, and for some regions in Canada.

In 1988, the US and Canada experienced their worst droughts since the 1950s. The cost of damages – to energy, water and agricultural systems as well as to the environment - reached US$39 billion in the US and US1.8 billion in western Canada.

India experiences one of the worst droughts of the 20th century. Rainfall decreases by an average of twenty percent. The greatest impacts seen in the dry northwest and in Pakistan. Around 285 million people are affected by food shortages.

A four-year drought starts in Siberia, lasting 42 months and covering over 3 million km2.

The southeastern US sees a shortlived but intense summer drought from June through August. Estimated losses from agriculture are US$1.3 billion. 100 human mortalities.

By 1985, a two-year drought in Ethiopia has caused widespread famine, taking more than a million lives.

Drought in Africa’s Sahel region causes widespread famine in 1984 and 1985, with 600,000 estimated human mortalities.

Drought in North eastern Africa correlates with a strong El Nino in 1983-84. Leads to reduced agricultural production and low streamflow in the Blue Nile river, affecting water supply for downstream nations.

1983 marks the onset of a severe two-year drought in Ethiopia that is the worst to hit the country in a century. Effects of the drought are worsened by conflict, government policy and conflict.

A two-year drought begins in Niger, which affects 62 percent of the national cattle herd.

1982 sees the onset of a prolonged drought in eastern Siberia. It lasts for 23 months and covers almost 6 million km2. Results in increase in wildfires.

A severe drought starts in New Zealand, lasting through 1983. Affects water supplies in Auckland.

Drought in Southeast Australia continues into its fourth year. Ranking as one of the worst to hit the country in economic terms, costs are around US$3 billion. Impacts on agriculture are severe, leading to rural unemployment. The Ash Wednesday fires in Victoria and South Australia claim 75 lives, as dry forest temperatures exceed 40°C.

Sub-Saharan Africa is plagued by severe drought in the early- to mid-1970s and throughout the early 1980s. Leads to widespread famine and hundreds of thousands of deaths.

In 1981, Botswana sees the onset of a three-year drought that depletes the national cattle herd by 20 percent.

From June to September 1980, an intense heatwave descends on the central and Midwestern US. Damage costs are estimated at around $US20 billion. 10,000 people die from drought-related impacts and heat stress.

Severe drought hits India. Rainfall is 19 percent below average levels and food production is cut by twenty percent.

Following an exceptionally wet decade, central and eastern Australia, and especially southeastern Australia, succumbs to a four-year drought starting in 1979.
